Postmodal Art

The composer can no longer be merely a composer of music - they must also compose with visuals, interactivity, improvisation, or other disciplines. This phenomenon, observable in all classical mediums, is largely due to the advances in technology available to the modern creative. It is no new occurence that a 12 year old equipped with the right software can compose music that rivals what currently tops the pop charts. The man with an iPhone can begin shooting a documentary at any moment at a reasonable level of production quality.


Academia must begin to look forward into these new integrative artforms in ways that equip the modern creative with usable skills. The in-vogue tools and mediums now evolve so fast that it is ineffective to focus on learning one tool in-depth. Tape music, which was very popular in the 1980s now lacks significant representation at the largest electronic music conferences - with many of these conferences now favoring interactive multimedia works. The most promising path forward is for the artist to attempt to link two or more disparate disciplines with the intent of deriving postmodal design principles that may be applied to any future medium that may become useful to them. This is similar to the concept of the "polyglot" in computer programming. It isn't the language you learn that is important, it's the concepts behind it.


My dissertation work outlines the connection between music and VR (a medium that encompasses countless other component mediums). Scholars such as Serafin, Kojs, et al. have mused on the connection between these two disciplines, but much is lacking in the specific area of music-first VR. The final manifestation of my dissertation work was a postmodal work titled Abstruse. I built this site to document Abstruse - scroll down the page to see it in action, and to learn more.
